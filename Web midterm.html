About Angkor Wat Temple

Angkor Wat Temple is one of the most iconic and mysterious temples in Cambodia, located at the heart of the ancient city of Angkor Thom. Built in the late 12th or early 13th century during the reign of Jayavarman VII, the temple stands as a masterpiece of Khmer architecture and a symbol of the powerful Angkor Empire. What makes Bayon truly unique is its countless serene stone faces. There are over 200 large faces carved into the temple’s towers, each believed to represent either the king himself or the bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara. These faces, with their calm smiles and closed eyes, gaze in all directions, creating a sense of quiet watchfulness and spiritual presence. Architecturally, Bayon differs from other Angkor temples. Instead of wide open spaces and grand symmetry like Angkor Wat, Bayon feels more compact and maze-like. Its narrow corridors, steep stairways, and layered terraces invite visitors to explore and discover hidden corners.
Another fascinating feature is the temple’s bas-reliefs. The walls are covered with detailed carvings that depict both historical events and everyday life in the Angkor period—scenes of markets, battles, festivals, and even cockfighting. These carvings provide valuable insight into the culture and society of ancient Cambodia. Originally built as a Mahayana Buddhist temple, Bayon later incorporated Hindu elements as religious influences shifted over time. This blending of beliefs reflects the dynamic spiritual history of the Khmer Empire. Today, Bayon Temple is a major highlight of the Angkor Archaeological Park and a must-see destination for visitors to Siem Reap. Its enigmatic smiles and intricate artistry continue to captivate travelers, historians, and archaeologists alike.

restoration

In the first part of the 20th century, the École Française d'Extrême Orient took the lead in the conservation of the temple, restoring it in accordance with the technique of anastylosis. Since 1122 the Japanese Government team for the Safeguarding of Angkor (the JSA) has been the main conservation body, and has held annual symposia.
